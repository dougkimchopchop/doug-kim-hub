Daily Writing Practice

Journaling has no reader in it. This is the daily loop for converting one raw entry into something written for a real person — and shipping it the same day.

5 reader-forcing techniques 1 daily loop 4-question finished check
THE LOOP

Run this once a day

Twenty to forty minutes, start to finish. The problem isn't craft — journaling has no reader in it, and technique alone doesn't put one there.

  1. Pull one raw entry — something already journaled, no new material needed.
  2. Name the reader — one real, specific person who doesn't already know what's in your head. Not "an audience."
  3. Pick one technique below and rewrite just the opening or the turn in that shape.
  4. Ship it, same day — send it to that person, or post it with zero ceremony. No editing pass, no sitting on it.
Finished means the technique landed — check the four questions at the bottom, not whether it's good.
01 · START HERE

Reader-Forcing Techniques

These don't just change the sentence shape — they make writing to yourself structurally impossible, because the reader is built into the grammar. Rotate through all five before touching anything else.

Baldwin — "A Letter to My Nephew"

Address one named person directly for the whole piece.

Test it Write the hard thing you'd only say to them, using their name.

Dawes — "Death"

Address the reader as "you" throughout, folding them into the experience.

Test it Rewrite a memory addressed to "you," making the reader complicit.

Bambara — "A Sort of Preface"

Breathless run-on sentences addressed to "you," claiming it isn't autobiographical.

Test it One long run-on sentence addressed to "you" about a fear of being found out.

Otsuka — "Buddha in the Attic"

Speak for a group's shared experience with a collective "we," without naming individuals.

Test it Write a scene using only "we," letting it hold many different details inside it.

Walker — "How to Make a Slave"

Narrate your own past self at a remove, as second-person imperative instructions.

Test it "Gather... Take... Realize..." — narrate a memory as commands to "you."
02 · ONCE THE READER IS REAL

Secondary Techniques

Use these to shape the piece further, after a reader-forcing technique has already done its job. Full list lives in The Craft Catalog.

Opening Baldwin, "Notes of a Native Son"

Dated scene, no warm-up, short facts building to emotion.

Opening Didion, "Goodbye to All That"

General "we" certainty eroding into a doubting "I."

Lists Diaz, "Top Ten Reasons..."

Numbered list, confident persona, never explain.

Sentence Messud, "The Road to Damascus"

One clause-stacked sentence, eight-plus clauses, no period.

Sentence Lahiri, "In the Sun"

"But" as a structural hinge every few sentences.

SHIPPING

Shipping Mechanics

  1. Where: a text to the named reader, a shared doc, a public note — the venue doesn't need to be impressive, it needs to exist outside your own head.
  2. When: same day as the rewrite. The gap between draft and out-the-door is what breaks the interior-voice habit.
  3. Cadence: daily journaling stays private. The conversion ships daily or at minimum weekly — don't let it pile up unshipped.
CHECK

Finished Checklist

  1. Did I name one real, specific reader?
  2. Does this tell them something they don't already know, instead of processing something I need to expel?
  3. Did the technique's specific move actually land — yes or no?
  4. Did I cut anything that only makes sense if the reader already knows my internal context?
All four yes? It's finished. Ship it.
